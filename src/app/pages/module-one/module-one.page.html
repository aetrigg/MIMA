The Science of Attention, Focus, and Concentration

1.1 Key to Performance: Focus and Attention

  • Focus is a central key to performance excellence.

  • Success in any endeavor is dependent on:
    1) Identifying the elements essential for success;
    2) Maintaining attention on the essential elements; and
    3) Ignoring the distractions.

  • In the stimulus rich environment of the O.R. attentional selectivity is critical because our attention capacity or working memory is limited.

  • Working memory is a cognitive system that temporarily stores information so that it can be accessed quickly, integrated with other information, or otherwise manipulated.

  • Working memory can aid one’s ability to focus on task-relevant information and ignore distractions.

  • Reflection: What are some situations in surgery that can overload your working memory?

  • Cognitive overload may negatively influence performance in a number of ways:
    1. Increased distractibility and decreased sensitivity to relevant information;
    2. Ability to process information is slowed, increasing our decision making rate;
    3. Capacity for verbal information is significantly decreased; and
    4. Limited amount of new information stored in long-term memory.

  • Reflection: Select a specific performance situation and identify the essential elements for success, and the most common nonessential distractions that you are likely to encounter. Feel free to consider any performance situation, or a specific phase or task within a complex procedure.

1.2 What's So Hard about Focus and Attention?

  • Different tasks require different focus. Optimal performance requires knowing where to focus, when to shift focus, and how to shift focus and maintain attention. This becomes particularly challenging when emotions become intense.

1.3 Understanding Focus and Attention

  • The key is to make certain that your attention is on what optimizes your performance for the task at hand.

  • Attention has two dimensions: it has direction – either inward (to yourself) or outward (to your environment); and width – where attention can be either narrow or broad. Different tasks require different focus of attention.

Inward Outward
Narrow Focusing on deep breathing, relaxing specific muscle groups, focusing on one productive thought Fine-motor physical procedure (e.g., making an incision or suturing)
Broad Self-awareness of emotions, making "executive decisions", determining possible outcomes Assessing operating room environment (e.g., arrangement of equipment and personnel)

  • Research suggests that the best surgeons are able to maintain narrow external focus while engaged in the physical tasks of surgery, and rapidly shift to broad internal focus while diagnosing and making choices in treatment and procedures. Rapidly shifting focus between different narrow/broad and internal/external elements is critical to perform complex tasks – such as those a surgeon is required to routinely perform in a modern OR.

1.4 Neurology of Attention and Focus

  • Over-reliance upon thinking is one of the most common hindrances to performance, when fluid, fine motor skills are required.

  • Reflection: Consider any fluid, fine motor skill you have used outside of surgery (e.g., playing the piano, dribbling a soccer ball, driving a car) or currently use during a surgical procedure or task that has become a routine or automatic behavior for you. What would happen to your performance if you thought about every detail required to perform this skill?

  • The prefrontal cortex or the thinking part of the brain (“upper brain”) is the center of executive functions, and is essential for intentional learning.

  • Subcortical areas (including the basal ganglia and the amygdala) (“lower brain”) are always on and respond to stimuli automatically and involuntarily. This part of the brain recognizes patterns and associations, and executes habitual routines and actions.

  • “Top down” neurology is critical to manage the impulsive reactions of the lower brain, and exercising logical decisions at specific points in a procedure.

  • “Bottom up” neurology is essential for rapid execution of fine motor tasks, pattern recognition, and managing attention and energy for long periods of sustained performance.

"Top Down" "Bottom Up"
Areas of the brain Pre-frontal cortex Subcortical areas, including basal ganglia and amygdala
Strengths

  • Center of executive function

  • Essential to master new skills and techniques

  • Makes cognitive decisions

  • Mindfully shifts attention

  • Overrides automatic responses to stress or fear

  • Neurologically fast

  • Recognizes patterns and associations, "intuitive"

  • Rapid execution of fine-motor tasks

  • Always on, automatic and involuntary

  • Requires less energy than top down brain

  • Highly responsive to emotions
Weaknesses

  • Neurologically slow

  • Requires a high level of energy and is easily fatigued

  • Cannot actually execute fluid, complex actions

  • Highly responsive to emotions

  • Can be emotionally hijacked by the amygdala

    • Restricts attention

    • Can trigger impulsive behavior

  • Modifying top-down processes usually involves the internal dialogue or “self-talk” that we call thinking. The inner brain and bottom-up processes are modified through images, rhythms, emotions and sensations that are experiential in nature.
