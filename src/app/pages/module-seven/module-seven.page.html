Module 7: Developing Routines

Developing Routines

“Focusing appropriately before, during and after the competition is one of the least practiced but most important skills for high performance...”
- Terry Orlick, Psyching for Sport

  • A routine is a unique recipe for each individual that combines thoughts and self-suggestions to optimize the top-down brain; plus familiar actions, well-chosen movements, imagery, and automated patterns to optimize the bottom-up brain. The goal is to achieve your Ideal Performance State for the immediate task at hand.

  • By detailing your routine, you optimize your choices in advance when you are calm, relaxed and can fully consider all options. This typically makes for better choices, and reduces the decisions that you have to make immediately prior to and during performance. When you simplify and reduce demands, you reduce stress.

  • The components of a routine set a clear “target” for a performer and eliminate distractions. Executing a clearly defined routine increases the consistency of high performance and strengthens a person’s confidence and sense of being prepared.

7.1 Developing Effective Routines

7.1 Developing Effective Routines

  • Identify situations: As we have discussed throughout this series, you want to begin by having a clear "target." Start by identifying those predictable, anticipated situations within a procedure where you know that you will need to be shifting your focus and attention.

    • For most performers, there are also predictable transitions within a performance. An athlete can anticipate transitions between plays, following timeouts, and between periods of play. There are also predictable transitions during surgical procedures, such as: switching operating roles during the procedure (operating surgeon vs assistant), switching techniques (open resection of specimen during lap colectomy), completing one procedure and moving to another one (when multiple procedures are done on the same patient), returning after taking a break during a long procedure, and debriefing after the procedure, just to name a few.

  • Clarify target for each situation: For each situation, you want to clarify your "target" for the transition.

    • What is your Ideal Performance State that you want to achieve after making the transition?

    • What is your ideal energy level as you begin the new phase of performance?

    • What is your ideal energy level as you begin the new phase of performance?

    • What is the most ergonomically sound position you should have during this procedure? (How do you want the patient positioned, monitor positioned, and table positioned?)

    • What are the initial actions you will take when you actually begin performing after the transition?

  • Clarify strategy for achieving your target- Now clarify your strategy for achieving your target for each situation that you have identified. Focus on things that are under your control, such as actions, suggestions, images, and aspects of the environment that you can adjust.

7.2 Tips for Optimizing Routines

7.2 Tips for Optimizing Routines

Here are some tips for optimizing your routines:

  • Use imagery to practice your routine so that it becomes an automated, bottom-up process.

  • Do what elite athletes do: make an audio tape of your pre-performance routine and include:

  • Music

  • Suggestions

  • Guided Imagery

  • Self Talk

  • Periodically review and consider refining your routine, especially following an exceptionally positive performance.
