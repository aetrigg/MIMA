The Science of Attention, Focus, and Concentration

1.1 Key to Performance: Focus and Attention

  • Focus is a central key to performance excellence.

  • Success in any endeavor is dependent on:
    1) Identifying the elements essential for success;
    2) Maintaining attention on the essential elements; and
    3) Ignoring the distractions.

  • In the stimulus rich environment of the O.R. attentional selectivity is critical because our attention capacity or working memory is limited.

  • Working memory is a cognitive system that temporarily stores information so that it can be accessed quickly, integrated with other information, or otherwise manipulated.

  • Working memory can aid one’s ability to focus on task-relevant information and ignore distractions.

  • Reflection: What are some situations in surgery that can overload your working memory?

  • Cognitive overload may negatively influence performance in a number of ways:
    1. Increased distractibility and decreased sensitivity to relevant information;
    2. Ability to process information is slowed, increasing our decision making rate;
    3. Capacity for verbal information is significantly decreased; and
    4. Limited amount of new information stored in long-term memory.

  • Reflection: Select a specific performance situation and identify the essential elements for success, and the most common nonessential distractions that you are likely to encounter. Feel free to consider any performance situation, or a specific phase or task within a complex procedure.

1.2 What's So Hard about Focus and Attention?

  • Different tasks require different focus. Optimal performance requires knowing where to focus, when to shift focus, and how to shift focus and maintain attention. This becomes particularly challenging when emotions become intense.

1.3 Understanding Focus and Attention

  • The key is to make certain that your attention is on what optimizes your performance for the task at hand.

  • Attention has two dimensions: it has direction – either inward (to yourself) or outward (to your environment); and width – where attention can be either narrow or broad. Different tasks require different focus of attention.
